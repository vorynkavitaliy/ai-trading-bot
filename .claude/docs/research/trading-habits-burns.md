## The 39 Trading Rules — Complete List

The book is divided into three sections. The rules below are reconstructed from available reviews, excerpts, and summaries. Where exact numbering is confirmed by multiple sources it is marked [confirmed]; where reconstructed from theme/context it is marked [inferred from section].

### Section 1: The Power of Habits (Rules 1–15) — Technical Basics

**Rule 1** [confirmed] — You win either by being often right, or by winning big when you are right. There are two paths to profitability: high win rate OR high reward-to-risk ratio. Most retail traders try to have both and achieve neither. Pick one and optimize for it.

**Rule 2** [confirmed] — Build your system on quantifiable facts, not opinions. "Buy the dip" is an opinion. "Buy when price pulls back to the 50-day SMA during an uptrend" is a rule. Feelings generate losses; quantified signals generate edge.

**Rule 3** [confirmed] — Look for low-risk, high-reward, high-probability setups. All three must align. A setup that is high-probability but low-reward is not worth taking. You need asymmetry.

**Rule 4** [inferred] — Trade price action, not predictions. React to what price is doing, not what you think it will do. Reactive technical analysis beats forecasting.

**Rule 5** [inferred] — Trade with the trend. The trend is the path of least resistance. Trading counter-trend requires more precision and carries more risk. Qualify the trend on a higher timeframe before entering on a lower timeframe.

**Rule 6** [inferred] — Trade breakouts from consolidation. When price exits a range and makes new highs (for longs) or new lows (for shorts), momentum traders get aboard the new trend. Breakouts represent the market's vote on direction.

**Rule 7** [inferred] — Buy pullbacks in uptrends, sell rallies in downtrends. Momentum traders who miss the initial breakout buy the first pullback for a lower-risk entry in the direction of the trend.

**Rule 8** [inferred] — Let winners run with a trailing stop. Cut losers short. Accept when you are wrong. The asymmetry between your average win and average loss is what creates positive expectancy. Most traders do the opposite — they cut winners early and let losers run.

**Rule 9** [confirmed — attributed to Linda Raschke] — Big gaps tend to lead to trends. A "gap and go" is often a freight train. Do not stand in front of it. Trade the gap continuation, not the reversal.

**Rule 10** [confirmed — attributed to Linda Raschke] — Smart money acts in the last hour. Strong closes matter more than strong opens. Watch the close for conviction. An asset that closes near its highs on high volume is being accumulated.

**Rule 11** [confirmed] — Above the 200-day moving average is where uptrends thrive. The 200 SMA is the border between bull and bear territory. Only trade longs when price is above it; only trade shorts (or go to cash) when price is below it.

**Rule 12** [inferred] — Filter out noise and capture the trend. A trader's primary responsibility during system development is identifying what is signal versus what is noise. The market will always zigzag. Your job is to stay on board through the zigzags.

**Rule 13** [confirmed] — Reversals build slowly. Trends do not turn on a dime. If you are betting on instant reversals, you are often early and often wrong. Wait for structural confirmation before calling a top or bottom.

**Rule 14** [inferred] — Trade high-momentum situations. Volume confirms price moves. When price breaks out on significantly above-average volume, the move has institutional backing and is more likely to follow through.

**Rule 15** [inferred] — Use moving averages to define trend direction and entries. The relationship between price and key moving averages (5 EMA, 10 EMA, 21 EMA, 50 SMA, 200 SMA) gives you both trend direction and specific entry/exit triggers.

### Section 2: The Foundation (Rules 16–27) — Money Management

**Rule 16** [inferred] — Always have a trading plan before you trade. A trading plan specifies your entry trigger, stop loss, profit target, and position size before you place a single order. Without a plan you are gambling, not trading.

**Rule 17** [confirmed] — Never lose more than 3% of total trading capital on your worst day. Limit yourself to no more than three concurrent trades, risking no more than 1% each. If all three go against you simultaneously, you are down 3% — painful but survivable.

**Rule 18** [confirmed] — The worse you are trading, the smaller your trades should be. Conversely, be at maximum position size during winning streaks. Scale up with demonstrated edge, scale down with demonstrated slippage or error.

**Rule 19** [confirmed] — Position size for the worst-case scenario, not the best case or the status quo. Assume the trade will reach your stop. Assume slippage. Assume the gap. Your position size must survive that outcome.

**Rule 20** [confirmed] — Find low-probability stop loss locations as carefully as you find high-probability setups. A well-placed stop — behind a key level that price is unlikely to reach if your thesis is correct — is as important as the entry itself.

**Rule 21** [inferred] — Never add to a losing trade. Adding to a losing position is the single most reliable way to destroy a trading account. You are averaging down against a trend that is proving your thesis wrong.

**Rule 22** [inferred] — All trades should end in one of four outcomes: small win, big win, small loss, or breakeven. Never a big loss. If you eliminate the big loss from your distribution, you create the conditions for long-term survival and eventual profitability.

**Rule 23** [inferred] — Understand that you only control entries, exits, position size, and risk. The market decides the outcome. Accept this asymmetry. Your job is to make quality decisions, not guarantee results.

**Rule 24** [inferred] — Never expose your account to more than 5% total risk at any one time across all open positions. This limits maximum drawdown from any correlated shock (news event, flash crash).

**Rule 25** [inferred] — Adjust position size for volatility. When ATR (Average True Range) spikes, reduce position size to maintain consistent dollar risk per trade. A standard lot in a low-volatility period carries much more risk during a high-volatility period.

**Rule 26** [inferred] — Track your expectancy. Expectancy = (Win Rate × Avg Win) − (Loss Rate × Avg Loss). If your expectancy is positive, take every signal your system generates. If it is negative, fix your system before trading live again.

**Rule 27** [inferred] — Always know your exit before you enter. You need three pre-defined exits: a stop loss level, a profit target or trailing stop rule, and a time stop (exit if nothing happens within N bars).

### Section 3: Mind Over Emotion (Rules 28–39) — Trading Psychology

**Rule 28** [inferred] — Trade hope for facts and emotions for rules. Hope is useful for personal goals; it is a disastrous guide in live trading. Replace every emotional decision with a pre-written rule.

**Rule 29** [inferred] — Price will go in the direction that causes the most financial and emotional pain to the majority. The majority is usually wrong in the long run. This is why breakouts and trend continuations surprise most people — they feel wrong at the time.

**Rule 30** [inferred] — Stubbornness, ego, and emotions are the worst trading indicators. The trader who is right and the trader who is wrong both feel confident in real time. The only arbiter is price. Follow price, not conviction.

**Rule 31** [inferred] — Be incredibly stubborn with your risk management rules. Give up zero ground on your stop loss discipline. Every exception you make "just this once" is the beginning of account destruction.

**Rule 32** [confirmed] — Never lose more than 1% of capital on a single trade. The 1% rule is not training wheels — it is your survival mechanism. It means you need 100 consecutive max-loss trades to lose your entire account.

**Rule 33** [inferred] — Take your initial stop loss to protect mental capital and time. A loss held in hope is not just a financial loss — it occupies your attention and prevents you from seeing other opportunities. Cut it, move on.

**Rule 34** [inferred] — Elite traders are nimble. They change direction when the evidence demands it, not when their ego allows it. Being early to recognize you were wrong is a skill, not a weakness.

**Rule 35** [inferred] — Education is your first priority before profitability. New traders who skip the education phase and go straight to live trading pay tuition to the market. The cost of education via books and paper trading is always lower than the cost of losing real capital.

**Rule 36** [inferred] — The trading system makes money in the long term; the ego loses money in the short term. System-generated trades produce consistent outcomes over hundreds of trades. Ego-driven overrides are random.

**Rule 37** [inferred] — It is hard to keep taking entry signals during a losing streak. It is also hard to miss a signal that goes on to be a big winner. Both of these psychological challenges are normal. The solution is a pre-committed system that removes the decision.

**Rule 38** [inferred] — Verify everything statistically before trusting it. Do not rely on anecdote, cherry-picked trades, or the advice of others without backtesting. Everything you trade should have a demonstrated statistical edge across hundreds of samples.

**Rule 39** [inferred] — Develop good trading habits until they become automatic. The goal is to reach a state where you follow your rules without internal debate. Discipline must become habitual, not a daily act of willpower.

## Buy Signal Strategies

Burns identifies several mechanical buy signal frameworks across his books:

### 1. Moving Average Crossover (Primary)
The core signal in Burns' approach uses two exponential moving averages of different lengths:

- **Fast MA crosses above Slow MA** = buy signal
- **Fast MA crosses below Slow MA** = exit / sell signal

Most referenced pair: **5 EMA crossing above 20 EMA** (used for Bitcoin and growth stocks). This is called the "flying eagle cross" in bullish mode. Characteristics:
- Fast signal — enters and exits trends quickly
- Works on asymmetry: creates big wins and small losses, not high win rate
- Confirmed by price closing above both MAs, not just touching

Burns' MA Ribbon uses five EMAs (5, 10, 20, 30, 50):
- When all five EMAs align in order (5 > 10 > 20 > 30 > 50) and price is above all of them: strong bullish trend — enter on any pullback to the 10 or 20 EMA
- When EMAs are disordered or converging: range-bound — avoid or reduce size

### 2. Golden Cross (Longer-Term)
- 50 SMA crosses above 200 SMA = major bull market entry signal
- Backtested over 25 years on US equities: mean average trade +21.35% profit, system returned +625% vs. +550% for buy-and-hold with significantly lower drawdowns
- For crypto: same logic applies on daily chart — 50 SMA crossing 200 SMA signals macro trend shift

### 3. Breakout to New Highs
- Price breaks above the highest high of the last 20 days (Turtle Trading System 1)
- Entry: when daily candle closes above 20-day high
- Stop: ATR-based stop placed below entry
- This feels like "buying too high" but new all-time highs can only happen after a breakout — that is where big trends begin

### 4. Pullback to Key MA in Uptrend
- Price is above 200 SMA (bull territory)
- Price pulls back to the 50 SMA (strong stocks) or 21 EMA (volatile uptrends)
- RSI reaches 30–40 zone (oversold on pullback within uptrend)
- Entry: close back above the MA after the touch
- This is the classic "buy the dip" made mechanical and rule-based

### 5. RSI + 200 SMA Combination
- Stock index or asset is above 200-day SMA (bull market confirmed)
- RSI drops to 30 zone on daily chart
- Entry: when RSI turns up from 30 in a bull market
- This is a mean-reversion buy within a trend

## Sell Signal Strategies

### 1. Moving Average Crossover Exit
- **Fast MA crosses below Slow MA** = exit
- 5 EMA crossing below 20 EMA: exit all longs
- No waiting for confirmation — the cross is the signal. Price must close on the other side, not merely touch.

### 2. Death Cross (Longer-Term Exit)
- 50 SMA crosses below 200 SMA = exit longs, go to cash or short
- Burns' primary macro filter: "exit holdings and go to cash when SPY closes under its 200-day SMA"
- For crypto: same principle — BTC below 200 SMA on daily = macro bear regime, reduce or eliminate long exposure

### 3. Turtle System Exit
- System 1 exit: price hits 10-day low during a long trade
- System 2 (longer timeframe): entry on 55-day breakout, exit on 20-day low
- These are mechanical, time-based trailing stops using the recent range

### 4. Trailing Stop via ATR
- After TP1 is hit, move stop to breakeven
- Trail stop at 2× ATR behind the highest close achieved
- Exit when close falls below trailing stop level

### 5. RSI Extreme Exit
- RSI reaching 70 = take profits on long trades (overbought)
- RSI reaching 30 = take profits on short trades (oversold)
- Not a reversal signal — a profit-locking signal

### 6. Time Stop
- If a trade does not move in your favor within a defined time window (N bars), exit regardless of P&L
- Burns emphasizes that a trade that "goes nowhere" is consuming capital and attention that could be deployed elsewhere

## Moving Average Systems

### The MA Ribbon (Burns' Custom Indicator)
Five exponential moving averages plotted simultaneously:

| MA | Period | Purpose |
|----|--------|---------|
| EMA | 5 | Momentum — most sensitive, shows current energy |
| EMA | 10 | Short-term trend direction |
| EMA | 20 | Mean reversion level, medium-term anchor |
| EMA | 30 | Swing filter — filters noise from entries |
| EMA | 50 | Pullback zone in strong uptrends |

**Bullish fan pattern** (all MAs in order, price above all): strong trend, buy pullbacks to lower MAs
**Converging / crossing MAs**: range-bound market — reduce exposure, wait for re-expansion
**Bearish fan pattern** (all MAs inverted, price below all): downtrend, only trade shorts or stay flat

### Crossover Pair Strategies
Burns documents the following pairs as historically effective:

| Signal | Entry | Exit | Characteristics |
|--------|-------|------|-----------------|
| 5/20 EMA | 5 EMA crosses above 20 EMA | 5 EMA crosses below 20 EMA | Fast, high frequency, low win rate, large R |
| 10/30 EMA | 10 EMA crosses above 30 EMA | 10 EMA crosses below 30 EMA | Medium-term swing trades |
| 50/200 SMA | 50 SMA crosses above 200 SMA | 50 SMA crosses below 200 SMA | Long-term position trades, macro filter |
| Price / 200 SMA | Price closes above 200 SMA | Price closes below 200 SMA | Regime filter only |

### Key MA Levels and Their Meanings
- **5 EMA**: momentum trailing stop (price falling below 5 EMA = momentum gone)
- **10 EMA**: "great MA to keep traders on the right side of the major trend"
- **21 EMA**: "last line of support in a volatile uptrend" — pullback entry zone
- **50 SMA**: defense line for strong uptrends — "where leading stocks pull back to"
- **100 SMA**: oversold level for corrections in bull markets
- **200 SMA**: the bull/bear border — the most important single MA for regime identification

### EMA vs SMA Preference
Burns prefers **EMA for short-to-medium timeframes** and **SMA for longer timeframes** (50, 100, 200):
- EMA reacts faster = better for catching early trend changes
- SMA at longer periods = cleaner, more respected by institutional money
- For the 200: SMA provides better bounce/resistance levels because more traders watch SMA than EMA at that scale

## Risk Management Rules

Burns' risk framework is structured around five core principles:

### 1. The 1% Rule (Core)
Never risk more than 1% of total trading capital on a single trade. Position size is calculated backward from the stop loss:
```
Position Size = (Account × 1%) / (Entry - Stop)
```
This means 100 consecutive max-loss trades would be required to lose the entire account. No realistic losing streak comes close.

### 2. The 3% Daily Maximum
Never lose more than 3% of trading capital on any single day. Maximum three concurrent trades at 1% risk each. If all three stop out simultaneously, the daily loss is 3% — survivable.

### 3. The 5% Total Exposure Rule
Never expose more than 5% of total trading capital to risk at any one time across all open positions. This protects against correlated moves (e.g., a broad market crash hitting all positions simultaneously).

### 4. Volatility-Adjusted Sizing
When ATR spikes (volatile market), reduce position size proportionally. The dollar risk stays constant; the number of units traded decreases. This is equivalent to maintaining constant R-per-trade regardless of instrument volatility.

### 5. Drawdown Scaling
- During winning streaks: trade at maximum allowed position size
- During losing streaks: reduce position size immediately
- Burns' personal target: limit annual capital drawdown to 5%
- If a losing streak begins, reduce size until you demonstrate positive expectancy again

### Additional Rules
- Never add to a losing position — ever
- SL must be placed at a technically significant level (not arbitrary percentage)
- The "low-probability stop" concept: place SL where price should NOT go if your thesis is correct
- Consistency Rule from prop firm context: no single trade should represent more than 40% of total profits (prevents lucky single trade from inflating statistics)

## Position Sizing Recommendations

Burns advocates for a fixed-fractional system with volatility adjustment:

**Base Model:**
- Risk per trade: 1% of account
- Max concurrent trades: 3 (if correlated) to 5 (if uncorrelated)
- Max total portfolio risk at any moment: 3–5%

**Volatility Adjustment:**
```
Adjusted Units = (Account × Risk%) / (ATR × ATR_Multiplier)
```
Where ATR_Multiplier is typically 1.5–2.0 for the stop distance.

**Scaling Rules:**
- Start small when testing a new system — use minimum size until 20+ trades confirm edge
- Scale up gradually as edge is confirmed
- The worse your recent performance, the smaller you should trade
- The better your recent performance, the closer to maximum size you can go

**For Prop Firm Accounts (directly applicable to our bot):**
- Burns' 1% per trade maps well to our 22.5% × 5× leverage structure when SL is ≤ 3% of account
- The key is that SL placement must create a max loss consistent with the account rules
- Our HyroTrader constraint of ≤3% per trade matches Burns' "never a big loss" principle

## When to Stay in Cash (No Trade)

Burns is explicit about the conditions that warrant staying completely flat:

1. **Price is below the 200-day SMA** — macro bear regime. For longs, the wind is against you. Go to cash or only trade short-side setups.

2. **Moving averages are tangled / converging** — range-bound market. Trend-following systems fail in chop. No confirmed trend = no trade.

3. **ATR is extremely low** (below 0.1% of price for crypto) — no volatility means no movement, trades get stuck, commissions eat profits.

4. **ATR is extremely high** (above 3% for crypto) — excessive volatility creates whipsaws that stop out even well-placed stops. Reduce size or stand aside.

5. **All recent trades are losers** — "the worse you are trading, the smaller your trades should be." Extend to: if you are on a losing streak, go to minimum size or flat until the market's character matches your system again.

6. **During major news events** — fundamental shocks invalidate all technical signals temporarily.

7. **No setup meets all criteria** — Burns emphasizes patience. Most retail traders overtrade. Waiting for only high-probability + high-reward + low-risk setups means trading less than most traders expect.

8. **System has not been verified** — do not trade live capital without backtested edge. If you cannot answer "what is my historical win rate and expectancy on this signal," do not trade it.

## Trend Following vs. Counter-Trend Rules

Burns is a trend follower, not a counter-trend trader. His framework:

### Trend Following (Preferred)
- Enter in the direction of the higher-timeframe trend
- Use pullbacks as entry points (lower risk than chasing)
- Trail stops to capture extended moves
- Accept more losses in exchange for occasional large wins
- Works best in trending markets (trending ~30% of the time)

### Counter-Trend (Avoided / Limited)
- Burns acknowledges mean-reversion signals (RSI at 30 in a bull market) but treats them as entries in the direction of the larger trend — not reversals
- True counter-trend trading (fading a trend) is described as higher-risk because you are fighting momentum
- The exception: "reversals build slowly" — once structural reversal is confirmed (e.g., price breaks below 200 SMA, 50 SMA crosses below 200 SMA), the new downtrend is tradeable in the short direction

### Market Regime Filter (Critical)
Burns uses a two-regime system:
- **Bull regime**: price above 200 SMA → only long setups
- **Bear regime**: price below 200 SMA → cash or short setups only
- This single filter eliminates the majority of catastrophic drawdowns that trend followers experience

## Handling Losing Streaks

Burns addresses losing streaks directly across multiple books:

### Mental Framework
- Losing streaks are inevitable. Even the best traders have 50–70% win rates — meaning 30–50% of trades are losers.
- A losing streak does not mean your system is broken. It means your system is expressing its natural variance.
- The psychological challenge: it is hard to keep taking entry signals during a losing streak. The solution is pre-commitment — you must decide before the streak starts that you will continue taking signals.

### Operational Response
1. **Reduce position size immediately** — not later, immediately when the losing streak begins
2. **Review trades for process errors** — were you following the rules, or were you deviating? Losses from rule-following are expected; losses from rule-breaking are problems.
3. **Do not try to "win back" losses** — revenge trading is the single most destructive behavior. Larger positions after losses = larger losses.
4. **Check market regime** — is the market in a state that is favorable for your strategy? A trend-following system in a choppy range-bound market will have a losing streak. The solution is to stop trading until the regime returns.
5. **Paper trade or reduce to minimum size** until you see a return to positive expectancy across 5–10 trades.
6. **Trust the backtested edge** — if your system had positive expectancy over 500+ historical trades, a 10-trade losing streak is within statistical normal distribution. Abandoning it at the worst moment is the most expensive mistake traders make.

## Summary: The Burns Framework in One Page

**Core Philosophy:** Replace opinions and emotions with quantified rules. Trade price action, not predictions.

**Entry Logic:**
1. Macro regime check: price above 200 SMA (daily) = only longs
2. Trend confirmation: price above all EMAs in the ribbon (or above 50 SMA on entry timeframe)
3. Setup type: either (a) breakout to new high, or (b) pullback to key MA in uptrend
4. RSI confirmation: 30–50 on pullback entries, any level on breakout entries
5. Volume confirmation: above-average volume on breakout candle

**Exit Logic:**
1. Stop loss: placed below the setup level (below OB, below key MA, below recent swing low)
2. Fast exit: fast EMA crosses below slow EMA
3. Trailing stop: 2× ATR behind highest close after TP1
4. Profit target: minimum 1.5× the distance to stop (1.5R minimum)
5. Time stop: if trade has not moved in 20 bars, exit and reassess

**Risk:**
- 1% risk per trade (dollar terms)
- Max 3% daily loss (3 trades × 1%)
- Never add to losers
- Reduce size during losing streaks
- Scale up during winning streaks

**When to not trade:**
- Price below 200 SMA
- MAs tangled / no clear trend
- ATR too low or too high
- Active losing streak (5+ consecutive losses = reduce size to minimum)
- Major macro news imminent
